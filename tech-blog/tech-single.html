Types of Digital Threats

07 April, 2024 by Jeniffer 2344
  • Share on Facebook
  • Tweet on Twitter

Do you know the main types of cyber attacks? Every year, the list of companies affected by hacking gains thousands of victims, generating losses that exceed financial losses. The truth is that mitigating these threats is a major challenge of the Digital Age — perhaps one of the biggest — and there is no way to evolve without knowing the enemy!

First of all, what is a cyber attack?

A cyber attack is a coordinated action aimed at gaining unauthorized access to data or IT systems. The ultimate objective may be the theft of information, extortion and even the paralysis of business activities. The numerous types of cyber attacks can affect various victim profiles: ordinary users, governments and companies. In the latter case, hackers go after confidential and valuable assets, such as customer data, financial information and intellectual property.

The 6 most common types of cyber attacks

Phishing: The term 'phishing' refers to a practice in which criminals send fake emails, which are crafted to appear authentic from legitimate companies, such as banks, online stores, or social media services. These emails usually include logos, design, and language similar to those used by real companies. Such messages often request sensitive personal information, such as passwords, credit card numbers, social security numbers, among others, encouraging recipients to act quickly.

Malware: short for "malicious software," refers to any type of software developed with the intent to cause damage, steal data, or control computer devices and systems without the user's consent. Here are some of the main categories of malware: • Viruses: They can corrupt or delete files and even render the system inoperable. • Trojans: Allow theft of information, installation of other malware, and unauthorized remote access to the system. • Ransomware: Encrypts files or locks access to the system, demanding ransom for restoration of access or decryption of files. • Spyware: Collects user information without permission, including browsing history and personal data, which can be used for malicious activities such as identity theft.

Social Engineering: Social engineering is a technique that involves psychological manipulation to persuade individuals to share confidential information or perform harmful actions. Typically, criminals employing social engineering often conduct thorough research on their victims before initiating an attack. This may involve gathering publicly available personal information, such as employment history, interests, hobbies, or social connections. With this data in hand, social engineers can customize their approaches to appear more legitimate and persuasive, thereby increasing the chances of success in their manipulation attempts.

Reverse Engineering Attacks: It's a technique in which attackers analyze systems or applications in search of security flaws or vulnerabilities. They may disassemble, decompile, or analyze the internal workings of a system to understand how it operates and identify potential weaknesses. Once these vulnerabilities are identified, attackers can exploit them to gain unauthorized access to confidential information, carry out malicious activities such as data theft or service disruption, or even compromise the integrity of the system by inserting backdoors or modifying its operation

Brute Force Attacks: It is a technique in which an attacker tries to discover passwords or encryption keys by testing all possible combinations until they find the correct one. This type of attack is extremely computationally intensive and can take a long time to be successful, especially if passwords are complex or long. Brute force attackers often use automated programs that sequentially try all possible character combinations, starting with simple passwords and moving up to more complex combinations. They can use dictionaries of common words, lists of previously leaked passwords, or even randomly generate the combinations.

Man-in-the-Middle Attacks: In this type of attack, an attacker positions himself between two communicating parties and intercepts their messages. This technique allows the attacker to read, modify or even inject new messages into the communication, without the parties involved noticing. This gives access to sensitive information, such as passwords, credit card numbers or personal data, that is being transmitted between parties. Man-in-the-Middle attacks are especially dangerous on public, unsecured Wi-Fi networks, where it is relatively easy for an attacker to infiltrate and monitor network traffic.

Alright, so after diving into that article, you start feeling like you've got a better handle on spotting those sneaky scams online, right? Now, it's all about putting that newfound knowledge to good use and making sure you don't get duped by the constant stream of scams flooding the internet. So, what's the game plan? Well, first off, you've gotta keep your guard up and follow all the tips mentioned above to avoid falling for scams
